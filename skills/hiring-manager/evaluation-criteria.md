# Evaluation Criteria — Senior Product Designer

This file defines what private sector hiring managers and design leaders actually look for when evaluating Senior Product Designer candidates. Use this as the benchmark for all critique sessions.

---

## What "Senior" Actually Means at This Level

A senior product designer is not just someone who has been designing for many years. Seniority is legible through:

- **Ownership**: Led the design of something end-to-end, not just contributed to it
- **Influence**: Shaped product decisions, not just executed them
- **Judgment**: Made tradeoffs and can articulate why
- **Scope**: Operated across features, flows, or platforms — not just screens
- **Autonomy**: Did not need to be told what to design — helped define what should be designed

If a candidate's materials only demonstrate execution (delivering wireframes, doing user research, building components), they read as mid-level regardless of title or years.

---

## What Hiring Managers Screen For (First Pass)

1. **Can this person work at our level of complexity?**
   - Enterprise, regulated, or platform-scale work signals this
   - Single-product or startup work can too, if framed around scope and ambiguity

2. **Will this person move fast enough?**
   - Signs of velocity: shipped work, iterative cycles, delivery under constraint

3. **Can this person communicate with PMs and engineers?**
   - Cross-functional language, not design-only language
   - Evidence of influencing decisions, not just presenting work

4. **Do they have a point of view?**
   - A senior candidate should have a design philosophy — implicit or explicit
   - Generic language ("I believe in user-centered design") is a yellow flag

5. **Is their portfolio legible to a non-designer?**
   - A portfolio that only a designer can interpret fails the hiring committee test

## Competency Areas and What Strong Looks Like

### Problem Framing
- Defines problems before jumping to solutions
- Asks what success looks like before designing anything
- Pushes back when the brief is incomplete or wrong
- **Strong signal**: Case studies that start with uncertainty, not a brief

### Systems Thinking
- Sees beyond the screen to the ecosystem, workflow, or organizational context
- Designs for reuse, consistency, and edge cases
- Works on design systems, pattern libraries, or IA — not just UI
- **Strong signal**: Language around structure, logic, taxonomy, flow — not just visuals

### Constraint Navigation
- Has worked under real constraints: accessibility, governance, legal, technical
- Didn't treat constraints as obstacles — treated them as design inputs
- **Strong signal**: Regulated environments, public sector, enterprise, or high-stakes platforms

### Decision Communication
- Can explain not just what they designed but why
- Has changed direction based on evidence or feedback and can describe it
- **Strong signal**: Bullets that include a decision rationale, not just an action

### Delivery
- Has shipped work — not just prototyped it
- Understands implementation well enough to work alongside engineers
- **Strong signal**: References to production, launch, or live systems

### Facilitation and Influence
- Has run workshops, alignment sessions, or critique cycles
- Has influenced product direction beyond UX boundaries
- **Strong signal**: Mentions of cross-functional collaboration with outcome language

## What Disqualifies a Senior Candidate (Immediate Filters)

- Resume reads like a list of Figma tasks
- No evidence of ownership — everything described as "contributed to" or "supported"
- Metrics that don't hold up to scrutiny ("increased conversion by 40%") without context
- Portfolio is purely visual with no problem framing or narrative
- Title is senior but scope reads junior (one feature, one product, limited cross-functional exposure)
- Inconsistent positioning (resume says enterprise, LinkedIn says freelance)
- Website or portfolio trying to be everything — no clear identity or specialization signal

## Private Sector vs. Public Sector Calibration

When evaluating for private sector roles (80% of target), hiring managers prioritize:

| Private Sector | Public / Regulated Sector |
|---|---|
| Speed to insight | Process rigor |
| Product intuition | Stakeholder management |
| Revenue / growth context | Compliance and accessibility |
| Cross-functional fluency | Documentation and governance |
| Autonomy signal | Institutional constraint navigation |

A candidate moving from public to private sector needs to translate their experience into private sector value language — not just describe what they did.

---

## The Differentiator Test

Ask: What does this candidate have that 10 other senior applicants do not?

Weak differentiators:
- "Experienced in Figma and design systems"
- "User research background"
- "Collaborative team player"

Strong differentiators:
- Early problem framing in ambiguous, high-stakes environments
- Governance-aware UX in regulated or enterprise settings
- Systems thinking at the intersection of content, IA, and interface
- Delivered in environments where most designers would struggle (political, constrained, fast-moving)
